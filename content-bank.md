    - |
      The single most important thing about bio bay tours that glossy tourism websites will not say clearly enough: if you book during a full moon, you are lighting money on fire. The bioluminescence — those tiny dinoflagellates that glow when disturbed — are completely washed out by moonlight. You'll paddle around in the dark, splash some water, see a faint shimmer if you're lucky, and spend the ride home wondering what all the fuss was about.

      I have guided groups during a full moon. I have also guided groups three nights after a new moon when the sky was pitch black. The difference is not subtle. It is the difference between "oh, I think I saw something" and "I cannot believe this is real."

      Puerto Rico has three bio bays: Mosquito Bay in Vieques (Guinness World Record, brightest in the world, 3-4 hours transit time, $230/person), Laguna Grande in Fajardo (most convenient, 50-60 minute drive from San Juan, $55-125, good but not dazzling), and La Parguera in Lajas (weakest brightness, 2.5-hour drive each way, BUT it's the only one where you can swim in the glowing water). Which one you pick depends on whether you value maximum glow or minimum logistics. For most visitors with limited time: Fajardo on a dark night. For the bucket-list experience: Vieques, and budget a full day.

  seasonal_notes: |
    San Juan has two seasons: dry (mid-December through April) and wet (May through November, with hurricane peak September–October). Dry season: highs 82–85°F, lows 72–75°F, minimal rain, steady trade winds. This is peak tourism — hotels are priciest, Old San Juan is crowded, restaurants need reservations. Wet season: warmer, more humid, afternoon thunderstorms that usually pass within an hour. September and October are the riskiest months for hurricanes — most of the time nothing happens, but when a storm hits (Maria 2017, Fiona 2022), it's catastrophic.

    The value sweet spot is May and early June: still mostly sunny, hotel rates drop, cruise crowds thin. Hurricane season technically starts June 1 but early-season storms are rare. Summer (July–August) is hot and humid with afternoon storms — Puerto Rican families take their vacations then, so there's lively local energy.

    For bio bay timing: new moon ± 3 days is mandatory for a good experience. Near full moon ± 4 days, don't book. Approximate 2026 new moons: June 15, July 14, August 12, September 11, October 10, November 9, December 9.
