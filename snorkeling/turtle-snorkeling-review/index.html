By Mateo Rivera — Mateo Rivera was born in Santurce in 1990, raised between the blue cobblestone streets of Old San Juan and the green foothills of El Yunque

I Didn't Expect San Juan, Puerto Rico to Feel Like This

Last December, I was guiding a turtle snorkel at Escambrón when a hawksbill turtle — rarer than the green sea turtles we usually see — swam directly under our group. Eight people floating face-down, completely still, watching this animal cruise past at arm's length. Nobody said a word for the entire encounter. When we surfaced, a woman from the group was crying — the kind where you've just experienced something you've wanted your whole life. Here's what matters: that was the $42 budget snorkel tour, not a $200 catamaran. Price doesn't always predict the quality of the encounter.

I've been guiding these waters since 2013 — more than 3,000 excursions, including hundreds of turtle snorkels at Escambrón Beach. My grandfather lived in Loíza and every Sunday he'd take me to the reef off Piñones. He'd float on his back, point at parrotfish, and tell me their names in Spanish. By the time I was 10, I could hold my breath for two minutes and identify a dozen species. "El mar no te debe nada," he'd say. The sea owes you nothing. Respect it and it'll show you things. I still think about that every time I'm in the water with a group.

San Juan has two seasons: dry (mid-December through April) and wet (May through November, with hurricane peak September–October). Dry season: highs 82–85°F, lows 72–75°F, minimal rain, steady trade winds. This is peak tourism — hotels are priciest, Old San Juan is crowded, restaurants need reservations. Wet season: warmer, more humid, afternoon thunderstorms that usually pass within an hour. September and October are the riskiest months for hurricanes — most of the time nothing happens, but when it does, it's serious.

The most excited I've ever seen a group was when an octopus decided to hunt directly below us at Escambrón. Eight tourists, all floating face-down in a circle, watching this octopus change color three times while it stalked a crab. Nobody moved for 15 minutes. When we got back to shore, a kid asked if we could "do the octopus tour again tomorrow." I had to explain that octopuses don't take reservations. The Moments That Made tours & excursions in San Juan, Puerto Rico Worth the Trip

My most memorable guided snorkel was with a 72-year-old woman from Minnesota who'd never put her face in saltwater. She was terrified. What Really Surprised Me About San Juan, Puerto Rico

After 13 years of guiding in these waters, here's what I've learned about San Juan snorkeling: the $42–$50 shore-based tour at Escambrón Beach delivers turtles at an 85–90% success rate on morning tours. You're in the water within 15 minutes of arriving. The guides know exactly where the turtles feed because they're at the same seagrass beds every morning. The $180 catamaran to Culebra is a completely different product — you're paying for the full-day boat experience, lunch, drinks, deeper water, healthier coral, and a beach that regularly makes "top beaches in the world" lists. Both are good. They're just not the same thing, and mistaking them for competitors at different price points is how you end up disappointed.

The thing that makes me angry: the party catamarans disguised as snorkel tours. Unlimited rum punch, DJ on board, "party atmosphere." Nothing wrong with a booze cruise — call it what it is. But these tours often stop at a mediocre reef for 30 minutes with half the guests too tipsy to swim properly. If you actually want to see marine life, this isn't it. Pay $60 for the party if you want the party. Pay for a snorkel tour if you want snorkeling. Don't pay for both and get neither.

I need to be direct about Escambrón's coral because too many tour listings say "untouched reef" and that's simply not true. Escambrón is a turtle and tropical fish experience. The coral is modest — some brain coral, some soft coral, but nothing like Culebra or the reefs off the west coast. If you're a certified diver or a serious snorkeler who's been to the Great Barrier Reef, manage your expectations. If you've never snorkeled before, you'll be thrilled. I've seen it happen hundreds of times.

Another surprise: how much afternoon weather affects visibility. After heavy rain, runoff clouds Escambrón for 24–48 hours. You literally cannot see your own fins. Push your booking back a day. Morning tours beat afternoon tours for nearly every activity in San Juan — turtles are more active, El Yunque is cooler, Old San Juan is less crowded, and the trade winds haven't kicked up yet.

Mateo Rivera's Insider Tips for Getting It Right

  • Book morning slots. Turtle sighting rates at Escambrón drop from 85–90% in the morning to about 60% in the afternoon. The turtles feed in the seagrass beds early. By noon, they're deeper.
  • Check the marine forecast. If seas are above 3–4 feet, visibility drops and the experience degrades fast. Free-cancellation policies are worth their weight in gold.
  • Bring contacts or your own prescription mask. Almost no snorkel operator carries corrective masks. I've seen too many people spend the whole tour squinting.
  • Eat where the locals eat. A mofongo that costs $22 near the cruise port costs $11 three blocks inland — and tastes better. If the menu has pictures of the food, you're paying for those pictures.
  • Uber cannot pick up at SJU airport's arrivals level. Walk upstairs to the departures level and request your ride there. Locals do this daily.
  • Book at least a week ahead during high season (December–April). The top tours — turtle snorkel, waterslide, food tour — sell out 3–5 days in advance.
  • Don't trust the first tour booth at the cruise port. Same tours, 30–50% markup for the convenience of booking on-the-spot.
  • If you wear prescription glasses, bring contacts or your own mask. I've said it twice because it matters that much.

What I Wish I'd Known Before I Went

In 2022, my neighbor's family from New Jersey decided to "save money" by taking the public ferry to Culebra instead of booking a tour. They showed up at the Ceiba terminal at 8 AM on a Saturday without reservations. The 9 AM ferry was sold out. The 1 PM ferry was sold out. They waited four hours for standby, got on the 1 PM ferry, reached Culebra at 2:30 PM, and had exactly two hours before the last return ferry at 5 PM. They spent more time commuting than on the beach and arrived back in San Juan at 8 PM, sunburned and frustrated. Book ferry tickets at least two weeks ahead at puertoricoferry.com, or just book a tour that handles the logistics.

A couple from Texas once told me their itinerary: El Yunque in the morning, drive to Fajardo for a snorkel trip at noon, then back to San Juan for a walking tour at 4 PM, then a food tour at 7 PM. I asked if they were training for something. They looked at me, confused. "We're on vacation," the husband said. "Right," I said. "So why are you working harder than your guide?" They canceled the snorkel trip. They later told me it was the right call — they actually enjoyed the two tours they kept instead of sprinting through four.

In 2023, I was at Luquillo Beach and started talking to a couple from Ohio on a cruise. They'd rented a car, driven themselves to El Yunque, and were now relaxing at the beach. It was 3 PM. Their ship left at 5 PM from Old San Juan. I asked if they knew about afternoon traffic on PR-3. They did not. I told them to leave immediately. They made it with 12 minutes to spare, red-faced and sweating. The lesson: assume traffic will add 30–45 minutes to any afternoon drive back to the port. Always.

If you're booking a turtle snorkel, here's the single most important thing: book the morning tour. I don't care which operator you pick — morning. The turtles are active, the water is calm, and the sun is at the right angle for visibility. Afternoon tours are a gamble I've seen lose too many times.

And one more thing: after heavy rain, skip the snorkeling. Runoff clouds Escambrón for 24–48 hours. You literally cannot see your own fins. Push your booking back a day. I've had to tell groups this mid-tour, and it's never a fun conversation. Better to reschedule than to float in murky water wondering what you're missing.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which San Juan turtle snorkeling tour has the highest turtle sighting rate?

Morning tours at Escambrón Beach consistently deliver 85–90% turtle sighting rates across all three operators. The San Juan Snorkeling Tour at Escambrón Beach has the largest sample size (16,432 reviews) and a 4.89 rating. Afternoon rates drop to about 60%.

Is it worth paying $99 for the underwater scooter snorkel tour?

It depends. The scooter covers about 3x the area, making it ideal for non-swimmers or older adults who tire easily. If you're a strong swimmer, the $50 tour will give you a similar experience for half the price. The 4.94 rating is based on 2,327 reviews — impressive, but a smaller sample than the 16,432-review tour.

What's the difference between the three Escambrón snorkel tours?

All three operate from the same beach, same seagrass beds, same turtle population. The differences are: group size (6–8 for the $42 tour, standard for the $50 tour, scooter-assisted for the $99 tour), cancellation flexibility, and propulsion method. Location does not differentiate them.

Is Escambrón Beach good for beginner snorkelers?

Yes. The water is calm, shallow (10–15 feet max), and the entry is gradual. Guides spend time with nervous swimmers. I've taken complete beginners here hundreds of times. Just avoid afternoons after heavy rain when visibility drops.

When is the best time of year for turtle snorkeling in San Juan?

December through April offers the most consistent conditions: minimal rain, steady trade winds, and calmer seas. Turtle populations are present year-round, but the dry season gives you the best visibility and lowest chance of weather cancellations.

Can I see turtles without booking a tour?

Yes, but a guide increases your chances significantly. The seagrass beds at Escambrón are accessible from shore, but knowing exactly where the turtles feed at different times of day requires local knowledge. The $42–$50 tours are worth it for the guide's expertise and the gear included.
